In 1999, a collector named Bruce Pascal spent $72,000 on a tiny pink Volkswagen van. It was a Hot Wheels car. Not a real car. A toy car. Small enough to fit in your palm. And at the time, it was the most expensive Hot Wheels car ever sold.

That record didn't last. The same car, a rear-loading Pink Beach Bomb prototype, later sold for $175,000. There are estimated to be fewer than 10 in existence. One collector reportedly turned down $300,000 for his.

## Why a Toy Car Is Worth $175,000

The 1969 Volkswagen Beach Bomb was one of the original Hot Wheels "Sweet Sixteen" designs. The first version had surfboards loaded through the rear window. It looked amazing. There was one problem: it was too narrow and top-heavy to work on the orange track. It would fly off every time.

Mattel redesigned it. Made it wider, moved the surfboards to side panels. The redesigned version hit shelves. The rear-loading prototypes were supposed to be scrapped. Most were. The ones that survived became the most sought-after Hot Wheels cars in existence.

## The Collector Market

Hot Wheels collecting is a $1 billion+ secondary market. There are roughly 20,000 different Hot Wheels variations dating back to 1968. The most valuable share a few traits:

**Prototypes and pre-production samples.** Cars that were never meant to leave the factory. The Beach Bomb is the most famous, but there are dozens of prototype colors and designs that command five figures.

**Redlines (1968 to 1977).** The original era. Named for the red stripe on the tires. A mint-condition 1969 Custom Camaro in an unpunched blister pack can sell for $3,000 to $5,000. Rare colors push that higher.

**Convention exclusives and limited runs.** Hot Wheels has been doing limited edition convention cars since the late 90s. Some of the rarest go for $1,000+ immediately on the secondary market.

**Treasure Hunts.** Since 1995, Mattel has hidden rare "Treasure Hunt" cars in regular cases. Super Treasure Hunts (STHs) have real rubber tires and spectraflame paint. Finding one in a store is like hitting a tiny jackpot. The secondary market values recent STHs at $20 to $100. Vintage ones go much higher.

## The $72,000 Question

Bruce Pascal has one of the largest Hot Wheels collections in the world. He's appeared on Pawn Stars. He's been profiled by Car and Driver, Getty, and SI Collectibles. The man literally wrote the book on rare Hot Wheels.

When asked if he'd sell his most valuable piece, he gave the same answer every serious collector gives: no.
