In 1999, a couple in Las Vegas was getting divorced. They couldn't agree on how to divide their assets. So they went to court. A judge presided. Lawyers were present. And the asset they were fighting over? Beanie Babies. They spread them out on the courtroom floor and took turns picking, like a playground draft for stuffed animals.

This actually happened. There are photos. A judge in a robe watching two adults crawl around on a courtroom floor sorting tiny beanbag animals into piles. It's one of the most perfectly absurd images of the 1990s.

## How Ty Warner Built a Mania Machine

Ty Warner launched Beanie Babies in 1993. They were simple: understuffed beanbag animals with short poems on their tags. They retailed for about $5. Nothing revolutionary. Except Ty Warner understood something most toy makers didn't: scarcity creates desire.

He kept production runs limited. He "retired" popular designs, announcing that certain Beanies would never be made again. He refused to sell to large chains. You couldn't buy them at Walmart or Toys R Us. Only small gift shops and specialty stores. This meant you had to hunt for them.

The combination of low price, perceived scarcity, and the retirement system created a speculative frenzy that made tulip mania look reasonable.

## The Numbers Were Insane

At the peak in 1998 and 1999:

- Ty Inc. was generating **$1.4 billion** in annual revenue. From beanbag animals.
- Beanie Babies accounted for **10% of all eBay sales**. The entire platform.
- The Princess Diana memorial bear, released after her passing in 1997, was selling for **$500 to $5,000** on secondary markets.
- Ty Warner became a billionaire. He was, briefly, one of the 400 richest people in America. From stuffed animals that cost about 50 cents to manufacture.

People weren't buying them for their kids. They were buying them as investments. "Beanie Baby price guides" were published like stock market analyses. People stored them in protective cases with tag protectors. The tag was everything. A Beanie with a bent tag lost half its value overnight.

## The Crash

It ended the way every speculative bubble ends: suddenly and completely.

Ty announced in late 1999 that all Beanie Babies would be retired. The market panicked. Then Ty reversed the decision (it was likely a marketing stunt). But the spell was broken. People realized a $5 stuffed animal was worth about $5.

The Princess Diana bear that people paid $5,000 for? Worth about $5 to $20 today. Entire collections that "appraised" for tens of thousands of dollars are now worth the cost of the storage bins they sit in.

## The Lesson

Every generation gets a collectible bubble. Baseball cards in the 80s. Beanie Babies in the 90s. NFTs in the 2020s. The pattern is always the same: genuine enthusiasm becomes speculation, speculation becomes mania, mania becomes a courtroom floor covered in stuffed animals.

The only people who made real money were the ones who sold at the peak. And Ty Warner, who made $6 billion total from Beanie Babies. He was smart enough to create the scarcity. Everyone else just fell for it.
